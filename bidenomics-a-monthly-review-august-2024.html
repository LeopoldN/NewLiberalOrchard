Liberal Orchard's monthly recap of key economic indicators like Inflation, Consumer Debt, and GDP

Woah woah woah welcome back sleepy Joe, it's been a while. Over a year since our last post actually, so lets get you up to date on what has happened.

GDP

Our favorite figure, GDP forecasts, released last thurdsay and the advanced estimate blew our minds. The U.S. economy expanded at a 2.4% annualized rate in the second quarter of 2023, according to the Bureau of Economic Analysis' third estimate. If we take the Atlanta Fed's GDPNowcast with a grain of salt, with their projected 3.5% growth in Q3, then 2023 is looking like an incredible year for Joe Biden.

The forcasted growth was primarily due to increased consumer spending, nonresidential fixed investment, state and local government spending, private inventory investment, and federal spending, which were partly offset by lower exports and residential fixed investment. Imports decreased.

Other key features one may want to focus on are an increase in real disposable personal income of 2.5% and an increase in the personal savings rate from 4.3% in Q1 to 4.4% in Q2.

Inflation

IMPORTANT NOTE: while this is the July 2023 report, inflation data for the current month won't be released until next month. Therefore, we will be viewing the June 2023 inflation report. July's data will be available in August's release.

Now, continuing the great news, the U.S. Consumer Price Index saw a light rise of 0.2 percent month over month, following a 0.1 percent increase in May, according to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. Comparing June 2022, we saw a year on year growth of 3 percent. It should be noted that June of 2022 is when inflation peaked in the US, and we have only seen progress since.

Core CPI (All items minus Energy and Food) rose on pace with All Items at 0.2 percent this month compared to last month and 4.8 percent compared to last June. While the Fed does not target core CPI with it's monetary policy, it is still a good sign that we could be experiencing a very rare soft landing (a reduction in inflation through rate hikes that does not coincide with high unemployment).

JOBS JOBS JOBS!

Just like last month and the month before that, and the month before that... America does NOT have a employment problem. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that total nonfarm payroll employment increased by 209,000 in June, while the unemployment rate remained little changed at 3.6 percent. Is the unemployment rate the lowest it is in history? No. But we have seen rates rise as high as 4 percent in the recent years and we have seen inflation lower and unemployment stay low. This is undoubtably a win for the Biden Administration.

Now for the abligatory "no, these jobs are not just returning from Covid (that was 3 years ago now)." As you can see in the chart below industry level employment has changed. This is not just people returning to their jobs. Agriculture is down 100k below precovid and Hospitality/Leisure are 400k below precovid. Meanwhile, Manufacturing is 200k above precovid and Finance is 300k above precovid. Obviously this is not people returning to work but shifting entire industries.

Secondly, when looking at the total number of employed people and the Prime Age Laborforce Participation Rate, we have still seen growth from the pre-pandemic highs.

Total employment is now a little above 4 million higher than it was pre-pandemic. If we only count those numbers as jobs Biden "created," he oversaw 133k new jobs every month since he took office. Thats good.

The last time Prime-Age Laborforce Participation (LFPR) was higher than today was 22 years ago. The economy is doing fine, and you can give Biden credit for continuing to oversee an economic expansion rather than try to discredit it.
