Bidenomics: a monthly review of the economy

Liberal Orchard's monthly recap of key economic indicators like Inflation, Consumer Debt, and GDP

By Liberal Orchard | June 30th 2023

Link copied!

GDP

The U.S. economy expanded at a 2.0% annualized rate in the first quarter of 2023, according to the Bureau of Economic Analysis' third estimate. This growth rate is higher than the 1.3% increase projected in the second estimate and follows a 2.6% GDP increase in the fourth quarter of 2022.

The upward revision in GDP growth was primarily due to increased exports and consumer spending, which were partly offset by downward revisions to nonresidential fixed investment and federal government spending. Imports, which subtract from GDP calculations, were revised down.

The report also included estimates of real Gross Output by industry. In the first quarter, the value added of private services-producing industries increased by 3.4%, government increased by 3.7%, and private goods-producing industries increased by 0.4%. In total, 16 of 22 industry groups contributed to the 2.7% increase in first-quarter real Gross Output.

Inflation

It is important to note that while this is the June 2023 report, inflation data for the current month won't be released until next month. Therefore, we will be viewing the May 2023 inflation report released this month. This month's data will be available in next month's release.

In May, the U.S. Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) saw a modest rise of 0.1 percent, following a 0.4 percent increase in April, according to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. When compared with May of 2022 the CPI has risen 4.0% this month.

Core CPI (All items minus Energy and Food) rose 0.4% this month compared to last month and 5.3% compared to last May. While inflation is still growing it has significantly slowed over the past few months coinciding with the Federal Reserve's interest rates.

Consumer Debt to Savings

A common talking point amongst Republicans is that the current economic "boom" we are experiencing is due to consumers taking out considerable amounts of debt to fuel their shopping. This fact is typically paired with conjecture and exaggeration about how eggs are $15 and people need to spend their whole paycheck on groceries. This is usually done mendaciously by showing the charts below.

Now obviously this looks horrendous! The Personal Savings Rate is near an all-time low at 4.6% and consumer credit card debt has jumped hundreds of billions! But this is meant to deceive you and make you think the world is falling apart. Ignoring the fact that the personal savings rate has recovered and has been recovering since the pandemic when one looks at Household Debt payments as a percentage of Disposable Income, we're not far off from pre-pandemic levels.

As you can see, other than the dip during covid (most likely due to the recession, spending cuts, and massive stimulus/debt payment suspensions), we are on the same trend as we were pre-pandemic.

JOBS JOBS JOBS!

Look, it does not matter what anyone says, the job market is (and has been) on FIRE. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that total nonfarm payroll employment increased by 339,000 in May, while the unemployment rate rose by 0.3 percentage points to 3.7%. Many conservatives will try to refute this claim by stating the Biden Administration did not "create" jobs and that these were simply people coming back to the labor force after the pandemic. This is simply not true or at least bad faith for a few reasons. First, during the pandemic, many people switched industries (as can be seen in the below chart). Therefore, while the leisure/hospitality industry lost ~400k employees since the start of the pandemic, the manufacturing industry added ~200k employees. That is not "people returning to work," that is entire industries creating 200k jobs that never existed before.

Secondly, when looking at the total number of employed people and the Prime Age Laborforce Participation Rate, we have still seen growth from the pre-pandemic highs.

Total employment is roughly 4 million higher than it was pre-pandemic, does that really sound like no new jobs were created? On the other hand, Prime-Age Laborforce Participation (LFPR) is at a peak last seen during the Clinton Administration. You may be asking, why should I care about Prime Age? What is labor force participation?

Well, in short, the LFPR is simply the total number of people in the labor force (employed/unemployed/seeking jobs) divided by the total civilian population. Prime-Age removes those younger than 25 and older than 54 from the equation. This is because economists believe that range is a person's "prime time" to be working. It removes those nearing retirement and those who are in high school/college from our numbers. I think most people would agree that measuring the number of people who are in their working years that are in the labor market is a much more telling metric.

Therefore, what we are seeing above is an extremely healthy labor force, with a participation rate above pre-pandemic levels. And no matter what any Trumpy says, we are doing great.
